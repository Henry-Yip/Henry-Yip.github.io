    Where does Momentum arise from?

    1 minute read

    Source of Momentum

    Since High School I was fascinated by the fact that momentum is simply a derivative of kinetic energy. I believe that this can be explained beautifully using the knowledge of more advanced classical mechanics.

    By Lagrangian Dynmaics, we know the equation of motion of an object is given by

    \[\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}}\right) = \frac{\partial L}{\partial q}\]

    where \(L=T-V\). Notice if the right hand side is equal to \(0\), i.e. \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q}=0\), we have \(\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}}\right)=0\), which implies \(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}}\) is a constant.

    This appears to be a constant in our equation of motion. What exact is it?

    Now set \(T=\frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2\) and \(V=0\), so \(L=\frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2\). Take \(x\) as the generalized coordinate (it is the only coordinate here), we arrive at \(m \dot{x}=c\)

    This is exactly the linear momentum of a system, which is known to be conserved due to Noether’s Theorem!

    Therefore, the energy of a system being independent of position arises a constant in the system, known as the (canonical) momentum. In other words, momentum is a term of freedom caused by the lack of a particular spatial coordinate. It is not conserved if the energy term involves spatial coordinates, say in the presence of electromagnetic waves!

    Side note: The other constant is known as the energy function and is given by \(h=\sum_i\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}_i} \dot{q}_i - L\). The reader can show that this corresponds to the energy of the system, which is \(T\)!
